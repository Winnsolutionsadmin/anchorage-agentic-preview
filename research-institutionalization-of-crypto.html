Executive Summary

The proof was the drawdown, not the rally

The strongest evidence that crypto has institutionalized is not the rally. It is the drawdown.

Through the first half of 2026, the digital asset market fell hard. Bitcoin traded near $67,000 to $68,000 in early June, total crypto market capitalization contracted roughly 20% in the first quarter, and May 2026 was the largest month of net redemptions from US spot Bitcoin ETFs in the year.[1][2] In a retail-driven, cycle-dependent market, that is the point at which ownership unwinds.

It did not. BlackRock's iShares Bitcoin Trust, the largest spot Bitcoin ETF, reported in its own first-quarter 10-Q that the bitcoin it held actually grew, from 770,792 to 783,744 BTC, even as its net assets fell from $67.4 billion to $53.4 billion over the same quarter.[3] The decline was price, not redemptions. That single primary-source fact is the thesis of this paper in miniature: the holders stayed.

Key Finding

Institutionalization is now a structural feature of this asset class, not a sentiment that rises and falls with price. It shows up where it counts, in who keeps holding when the market falls.

This paper makes four evidenced claims and then takes a position.

Ownership has broadened and stuck. Institutional ownership of spot Bitcoin ETF assets has roughly doubled year over year, advisors now drive the majority of reported holdings, and nearly three-quarters of surveyed institutions plan to increase allocations in 2026, even into the drawdown.[4][5][6]
The market re-sorted toward size and quality. Large-cap crypto has decisively outperformed small-cap, market capitalization has concentrated in a handful of liquid, regulated, benchmarkable assets, and the long tail has collapsed. This is the signature of institutional, not retail, capital.[7][8]
Crypto is becoming its own asset class, not a leveraged Nasdaq. Its correlation to equities stepped up structurally after the ETFs and is regime-dependent, but it decoupled sharply during the late-2025 and 2026 selloff. Crypto trades on its own drivers now, and it has acquired the factor architecture of a mature asset class.[9][10][11]
The asset became infrastructure. Federal charters, a stablecoin law, custody migrating onto chartered banks, and stablecoins settling trillions in real volume have turned crypto from an instrument you hold into rails you run on.[12][13][14]

Then the position. The next phase of this market is not another asset. It is a new kind of participant: autonomous software agents that hold balances, make payments, and act on institutional capital. The identity and payment standards being built for that world are real and converging fast, but they stop, by their own authors' design, short of the things regulated finance is built on: underwriting the actor, custodying the assets, and standing behind the controls.[15][16] Those are banking functions. The institutions that deployed capital into regulated, custodied crypto infrastructure will demand the same before they let an agent touch it. Anchorage's view is that the controls are the on-ramp, not the brake, and that Know Your Agent, done from inside a federally chartered trust bank, is the layer the standards leave open.

01 · Structural, and the proof is the drawdown

Institutionalization is now structural

The retail thesis of crypto held that ownership was reflexive: it climbed with price and evaporated when price fell. The 2026 data breaks that thesis.

Start with the holders. More than 1,680 distinct institutions reported holding BlackRock's IBIT in regulatory filings, including names like Goldman Sachs, Millennium, Jane Street, and Citadel.[17] Institutional ownership of spot Bitcoin ETF assets rose to roughly 38% by the end of 2025, up from about 24% a year earlier, per Bloomberg Intelligence data compiled from 13F filings.[4] Registered investment advisors, the channel that intermediates ordinary wealth, accounted for 50% to 60% of reported 13F exposure across 2025, per CoinShares.[5] This is not a hedge-fund trade. It is the wealth-management system adopting an asset class.

The intent data points the same way, and it was gathered into the downturn. In the Coinbase and EY-Parthenon 2026 institutional survey of 351 investors, 73% said they planned to increase digital-asset allocations in 2026, 66% already held exposure through spot ETFs, and the share of firms allocating more than 5% of assets under management to digital assets was projected to rise from 18% to 29% within the year.[6] Allocators do not deepen exposure to an asset they consider a passing retail mania.

The marquee channels opened, too. Morgan Stanley, the first wirehouse to authorize its roughly 15,000 advisors to actively solicit spot Bitcoin ETFs, went on to launch its own bank-issued spot Bitcoin product in April 2026.[18] When the largest US wealth-distribution network builds its own access, the question has shifted from whether to hold to how much.

Maturity also looks like two-sided behavior, and 2026 delivered that. A sovereign wealth vehicle, Abu Dhabi's Mubadala, continued accumulating IBIT through the first quarter.[19] A US public pension, Wisconsin's investment board, exited entirely.[20] The largest US academic endowment, Harvard, trimmed its position by roughly 43% but kept the Bitcoin ETF as its single largest disclosed listed holding.[20] Some bought, some sold, some rebalanced. That is what an institutional asset class looks like. It is not what a bubble looks like.

Key Finding

The signature of institutionalization is not unanimity of conviction. It is the presence of disciplined, disclosed, two-sided institutional behavior, persisting through a price decline.

None of this would have been possible without a change in the rules, and that change is itself evidence of institutionalization. In a thirteen-month window, the United States rescinded the accounting treatment that kept crypto off bank balance sheets (SAB 122, replacing SAB 121, January 2025), issued an executive order directing federal support for digital assets (EO 14178, January 2025), affirmed that national banks may custody crypto and removed the supervisory non-objection gate (OCC Interpretive Letter 1183, March 2025), dismissed its flagship enforcement action against Coinbase (February 2025), and approved generic listing standards that compressed crypto ETP time-to-market from many months to roughly sixty to seventy-five days (September 2025).[21][22][23][24][25] The plumbing was rebuilt. Capital followed the plumbing.

02 · The fingerprint of institutional capital

The market re-sorted toward size and quality

If institutional capital had truly arrived, it would leave a fingerprint, and the fingerprint is unmistakable: a flight to size, liquidity, and benchmarkability.

Over three years, the MarketVector Digital Assets 100 Large-Cap Index returned roughly +97% while its Small-Cap counterpart returned roughly -69%; over five years the gap is wider still, with the small-cap index sitting near multi-year lows.[7] The small-cap gauge offered no diversification benefit on the way down, retaining a correlation near 0.9 to large-cap crypto while badly underperforming it.[26] The "alt season" thesis, that capital rotates down the risk curve into the long tail, did not survive contact with institutional flows.

Concentration tells the same story. Bitcoin dominance ended the first quarter of 2026 at 55.8% of total crypto market capitalization, with Ethereum at 9.3%, so the two largest assets alone accounted for roughly 65% of the asset class.[8] Bitcoin represented 64.9% of the combined market cap of the top ten cryptoassets, down from 87% in 2014, with regulated stablecoins now a structural 11.6% share of that top ten.[27] The top ten assets hold roughly four-fifths of all crypto market value.[27] Capital concentrated where institutions can actually operate: the liquid, regulated, indexable top of the market.

Key Finding

Institutional capital does not buy the whole market. It buys the part of the market that is liquid enough to enter and exit at scale, regulated enough to custody, and benchmarkable enough to report. In 2024 through 2026, that part won, and the rest collapsed.

This re-sorting is precisely what factor research detects, and it is worth being clear about what is and is not proprietary here. The inversion of the crypto size premium, from a historical small-cap tilt toward large-cap outperformance, is observable in public index data (MarketVector) and in the published academic record on crypto factors.[7][28] It is also, independently, what Anchorage's internal factor research has measured. We reference that internal corroboration without publishing its figures; the public record alone establishes the point.

The deeper reading is the one Nathan McCauley has made publicly: the spot ETF did not create demand, it unlocked latent demand that already existed. The 2026 data refines that claim. The demand that the ETF unlocked is discriminating. It is concentrating in the assets that institutions can hold inside regulated structures, and it is leaving the rest behind.

03 · Its own drivers now

Crypto is becoming its own asset class, not a leveraged Nasdaq

A common shorthand holds that bitcoin is simply a high-beta proxy for the Nasdaq, a risk-on bet that rises and falls with technology equities at an amplified rate. The evidence supports a more precise, and more interesting, conclusion.

The structural floor of crypto's correlation to equities did rise with institutionalization. Academic regression on daily returns found bitcoin's correlation to the Nasdaq 100 climbed from roughly -0.10 before 2021 to an annual high near 0.76 in 2024, reaching a 180-day rolling peak of 0.87 after MicroStrategy entered the Nasdaq 100, the highest in the 2018 to 2025 sample, and the paper attributes the rise to institutional adoption rather than to transient risk sentiment.[9] Institutionalization tied crypto more tightly to the broader market. That is real.

But the linkage is regime-dependent, not a fixed beta, and it broke when it mattered. Coin Metrics put bitcoin's market beta to the S&P 500 well above 1 through much of 2024 and trending lower into 2025.[10] After the April 2025 tariff selloff, Kaiko found bitcoin's 90-day correlation to the Nasdaq fell to 0.27, its lowest since September 2024, before recovering to around 0.42.[29] By early December 2025, as bitcoin fell roughly 27% from its October peak while the Nasdaq 100 sat about 2% off record highs, the 20-day correlation went negative, to -0.43.[11] K33 found that bitcoin's beta actually declined during the strongest equity rallies, the opposite of what a leveraged-Nasdaq framing predicts.[30]

Key Finding

Crypto's correlation to equities is structurally elevated versus the pre-2021 era but regime-dependent, and it decoupled sharply in the 2025 to 2026 drawdown. Crypto is not a leveraged technology-equity proxy. It is becoming an independent institutional asset class with its own risk drivers.

That independence is not only a correlation story. It is a structural one. The academic literature now documents a stable factor architecture for crypto returns that mirrors mature asset classes: a three-factor model of market, size, and momentum prices the cross-section of returns (Liu, Tsyvinski, and Wu, Journal of Finance, 2022); downside beta is a separately priced risk (Journal of Banking and Finance, 2021); a machine-learning trend factor survives transaction costs and is not subsumed by the others (Journal of Financial and Quantitative Analysis, 2025); and blockchain-fundamentals factors price returns at least as well as return-based factors (2023).[28][31][32][33] An asset class with a published, peer-reviewed factor structure is an asset class that institutions can model, hedge, and allocate to on their own terms. This is the analytical lineage Anchorage's own factor research sits within.

04 · From asset to infrastructure

The asset became something you build on

The clearest sign that an asset class has institutionalized is that it stops being only something you hold and becomes something you build on. By 2026, crypto had crossed that line.

Charters

Anchorage Digital became the first crypto-native firm to operate a national trust bank charter from the Office of the Comptroller of the Currency, in 2021.[12] In December 2025, the OCC conditionally approved five more crypto-native national trust charters in a single action, for Circle, Ripple, BitGo, Fidelity Digital Assets, and Paxos.[34] The federal banking system is absorbing digital assets into its own perimeter.

A stablecoin law

The GENIUS Act, the first US federal framework for payment stablecoins, was signed into law on July 18, 2025 as Public Law 119-27, on bipartisan votes (Senate 68-30, House 308-122).[13] It requires full reserve backing in cash and short-term Treasuries, monthly public reserve disclosures, and limits issuance to regulated entities. The OCC issued its implementing proposed rule in February 2026.[35] A market-structure bill, the CLARITY Act, advanced through Senate Banking on a bipartisan 15-9 vote in May 2026, though it is not yet law.[36]

Custody migrating onto chartered banks

BlackRock named Anchorage Digital Bank an eligible custodian for its spot crypto exchange-traded products, extending a relationship that already custodied BlackRock's tokenized BUIDL fund.[37] The asset manager 3iQ selected Anchorage to custody and run infrastructure for six exchange-traded products listed on the Toronto Stock Exchange.[38] U.S. Bank, a custodian with more than $11.7 trillion in assets under custody and administration, was selected to hold the reserves backing Anchorage's payment stablecoins, a structure that pairs a traditional custodian with a chartered crypto bank.[14] Anchorage's Atlas settlement and collateral network scaled roughly fourfold in a year to nearly 600 participants.[39] Regulated capital wants regulated custody, and it is moving there.

Stablecoins as settlement infrastructure

This is where the rails are most visible. Circle reported USDC in circulation grew 28% year over year to $77 billion in the first quarter of 2026, with on-chain transaction volume up 263% to $21.5 trillion, surpassing USDT's volume for the first time since 2019.[40] Chainalysis estimated $28 trillion in real, adjusted stablecoin economic volume in 2025, growing at a 133% annual rate since 2023.[41] The institutional use cases are now concrete: Western Union launched USDPT, a federally regulated dollar stablecoin issued by Anchorage Digital Bank on Solana, as a 24/7 settlement alternative to SWIFT across its network of more than 550,000 agent locations.[42] State Street and Galaxy launched a tokenized liquidity fund that settles in PayPal's stablecoin.[43] Visa and Mastercard are settling and routing in stablecoins directly.[44]

The honest exception: tokenization

Not every part of this story is ahead of schedule, and a credible institutional paper should say so. Total on-chain tokenized real-world assets, excluding stablecoins, stood at roughly $31.5 billion in June 2026, with tokenized US Treasuries at about $14.8 billion, concentrated in a handful of institutional funds, and the category was roughly flat over the prior thirty days.[46] Tokenized equities trade at under 1% of the volume of their traditional-market equivalents.[47] Against McKinsey's conservative 2030 base case of $2 trillion to $4 trillion, and Citi's $5.5 trillion, the realized figure is one to two orders of magnitude smaller.[48][49] The direction of tokenization is not in doubt. Its realized business model and secondary-market liquidity are not yet here. Building the rails is necessary; assuming the volume will arrive on its own is not warranted by the data.

Key Finding

Institutionalization is no longer a flow story. It is an infrastructure story. The assets that matter are now custodied by chartered banks, settled in regulated stablecoins, and governed by federal law. The exception, tokenization of assets beyond cash, marks where the infrastructure is built but the volume has not yet followed.

05 · A new kind of participant

The next frontier is the autonomous agent

Everything above describes capital and infrastructure. The next phase introduces a new actor: the autonomous agent.

The economic case is being made by the most conservative institutions in finance. McKinsey estimates agentic AI could reduce banking operating costs by up to 20%, open a four-percentage-point return-on-tangible-equity gap between leaders and laggards, and put as much as 10% of the industry's roughly $1.2 trillion profit pool at risk over five to ten years.[50] Citi argues agentic AI could affect finance more than the internet did, and names financial services the second-largest consumer of generative AI.[51] Gartner projects that 40% of enterprise applications will embed task-specific AI agents by the end of 2026, up from under 5% in 2025.[52] Boston Consulting Group finds agentic AI has reached 35% enterprise adoption in two years.[53]

A discipline note belongs here, because the same sources that size the opportunity also flag the risk. Gartner predicts that more than 40% of agentic AI projects will be canceled by the end of 2027, citing cost, unclear value, and weak risk controls.[54] The agent economy is arriving, and it is arriving unevenly. The firms that govern it well will be the ones whose agents are allowed near real money.

That is not hypothetical, because agents are already transacting. Coinbase's x402 payment protocol crossed more than 100 million cumulative agent transactions on the Base network in roughly three quarters, with the transaction mix shifting decisively toward real economic payments.[55] In April 2026, x402 moved to neutral governance under the Linux Foundation, with founding members spanning Circle, Visa, Mastercard, American Express, Google, Microsoft, Amazon Web Services, Stripe, Cloudflare, Shopify, and the Solana and Polygon ecosystems; Google made it the default stablecoin rail in its agentic payments protocol.[56] Ethereum's ERC-8004 standard for agent identity went live on mainnet in January 2026, authored by contributors from MetaMask, the Ethereum Foundation, Google, and Coinbase.[57] Mastercard completed its first live agentic payment in September 2025 and rolled the capability across all US cardholders by November.[58] Analysts project US agentic commerce revenue could reach $1 trillion by 2030 (ICSC and McKinsey) and account for 10% to 20% of online retail (Morgan Stanley).[59][60]

The trajectory is clear. Agents will not stop at retail microtransactions. They will hold balances, manage collateral, execute against portfolios, and act on institutional capital.
06 · The load-bearing observation

The gap the standards leave open

Here is the load-bearing observation of this paper. The standards being built for the agent economy are good, they are converging, and they were deliberately designed to stop short of the functions regulated finance depends on.

Consider what the standards actually cover. The MIT Media Lab's foundational January 2025 work proposes authenticated, authorized, auditable delegation to AI agents by extending existing identity protocols, and it does not even use the phrase "Know Your Agent."[61] Skyfire positions itself as a leader of Know Your Agent, issuing signed credentials that bind an agent to a verified human and its permissions.[62] Experian launched its own Know Your Agent framework using Skyfire's protocol as the identity layer.[63] The identity-verification incumbents Sumsub and Trulioo both use the same three-word label, and both scope it explicitly to identity, authorization, and auditability, and explicitly not to underwriting, credit decisions, or asset custody.[15] Ethereum's ERC-8004 defines identity, reputation, and validation registries and, in its authors' own words, "does not cover payments," leaving application logic off-chain.[16] Visa's Trusted Agent Protocol confirms that an agent is authorized to act for a consumer; it is authentication, not custody.[64]

Two facts follow. First, Know Your Agent is a contested category, not any single firm's coinage, with academic, vendor, payments-network, and protocol claimants all using the term. Anchorage does not own it, and this paper does not claim otherwise. Second, and more important, every one of these standards answers the same two questions, "who is this agent, and what is it allowed to do," and none of them answers the three questions that a regulated institution must answer before it lets an agent touch its capital: who underwrites the agent, who custodies the assets it acts on, and who is accountable when it fails. As of June 2026, no financial regulator has issued a Know Your Agent standard.[15]

Key Finding

The agent-economy standards establish identity and authorization. They do not, by design, establish underwriting, custody, or accountability. That gap is not a flaw in the standards. It is a banking function the standards correctly leave to banks.

07 · The position

Know Your Agent, from inside a regulated bank

The pattern of the last three years is the argument for the next three.

Institutional capital did not flow into crypto until it could be held in regulated, custodied, benchmarkable structures. When those structures arrived, the capital came, and it stayed through a drawdown. The agent economy will follow the same path. Institutions will not deploy autonomous agents against real capital until the agent can be underwritten, the assets it touches can be custodied under governed controls, and a regulated entity stands behind the outcome.

That is the regulated-bank reading of Know Your Agent. It is not the identity layer, which the standards already provide. It is the layer above and beneath the identity layer: underwriting the agent before it transacts, custodying the assets it acts on with segmented, governed, recoverable controls (an allowance, not the keys), and carrying the accountability that a charter requires. Anchorage's position is that these controls are not friction on the agent economy. They are its on-ramp. An agent that a regulated bank can stand behind is an agent that an institution can actually use.

This is the continuation of a single thesis. Crypto institutionalized because the regulated layer made it usable at scale. The agent economy will institutionalize the same way, or it will stall in the same place tokenization has, with the rails built and the institutional volume waiting on the trust layer.

The recommendation is direct. Institutions preparing to deploy capital-bearing agents should require a regulated trust layer underneath them, and the firms building agent infrastructure should build on one. The identity standards are necessary. They are not sufficient.

Sufficiency is a bank.
Methodology

How this paper was built

This paper is built on public data, gathered and source-tagged in June 2026. Every quantitative claim carries a numbered citation with a source and an as-of date. Sources are confined to the class an institution can verify independently: primary regulatory and corporate filings (SEC, OCC, CFTC, the White House, Congress, company 10-Qs, 8-Ks, 13Fs, and official press releases), peer-reviewed academic literature, named data providers (CoinGecko, rwa.xyz, Chainalysis, CoinShares, MarketVector, Fintel, Coin Metrics, Kaiko), and named institutional research (McKinsey, Citi, BCG, Gartner, Fidelity, a16z, Grayscale, Coinbase Institutional).

Where figures rest on secondary reporting, that is stated and the figure is softened or attributed. Cumulative spot Bitcoin ETF net-flow figures show genuine cross-source variance in the $55 billion to $58 billion range because the standard flow tracker was not directly accessible; this paper therefore cites per-fund primary filings rather than a precise cumulative aggregate. Anchorage's own first-mover and "America's first" claims are presented as Anchorage's characterizations. Anchorage's internal factor research is referenced as independent corroboration of publicly observable findings, and its proprietary figures are not published.
