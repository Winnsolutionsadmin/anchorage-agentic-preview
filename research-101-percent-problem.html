Anchorage Research · Paper 03

The 101% Problem

Tokenization is inevitable. Its business model is not.

Pillar 3 — Capital markets (Atlas / agentic settlement). An Anchorage research paper, prepared for Nathan McCauley / Anchorage Digital Research.

Thesis
The argument

Tokenized rails are vastly cheaper to run. But until the old system is switched off, issuers pay for both. The cost is not 1%. It is 101%. That gap, not the technology, is what is holding tokenization back.

Executive Summary

Tokenization is the most over-forecast and under-realized idea in capital markets. The direction is not in doubt: moving assets onto programmable rails is cheaper, faster, and available around the clock. The problem is the transition. As long as the existing settlement, custody, and compliance machinery has to keep running alongside the new rails, an issuer does not pay 1% to tokenize. It pays 101%, the cost of the new system on top of the old one it cannot yet switch off. That is the gap between the trillion-dollar forecasts and the billions actually on-chain, and it is an honest thing for a regulated institution to say out loud.

Key Finding

Roughly $31 billion of real-world assets are tokenized on-chain today, against institutional 2030 forecasts of $2 trillion to $5.5 trillion. The gap is not a technology gap. It is a transition-cost and liquidity gap.

Section 1

The realized market is small, and roughly flat

The data is sobering against the hype. Total on-chain tokenized real-world assets, excluding stablecoins, stood at roughly $31.5 billion in June 2026, with tokenized US Treasuries at about $14.8 billion, and the category was essentially flat over the prior thirty days.1 The Treasury segment is concentrated in a handful of institutional funds, with Circle, BlackRock's BUIDL, Ondo, and Franklin Templeton accounting for the bulk of it.1 The starkest gap is in tokenized equities, which trade at under 1% of the volume of their traditional-market equivalents despite headline launches.2

This is not failure. The sector grew more than 250% over the prior fifteen months.2 But it is a fraction of what the forecasts imply.

Section 2

The forecast gap is real, and worth naming

McKinsey's conservative institutional base case puts total tokenized market value at roughly $2 trillion by 2030, around $4 trillion in an optimistic case, and states plainly that broad adoption is "still far away."3 Citi's base case is higher, at $5.5 trillion.4 Against roughly $31 billion realized, that is a gap of one to two orders of magnitude.1

Key Finding

A credible institutional voice should hold both truths at once: tokenization's direction is inevitable, and its current realized volume is one to two orders of magnitude below the forecasts. Stating the gap is what separates a bank's research from a vendor's brochure.

Section 3

Why the gap exists: the 101% problem

The gap is not because the technology does not work. It is because the transition is expensive. An issuer that tokenizes an asset does not get to retire its existing transfer agent, custodian, reconciliation, and compliance stack on day one. It runs the new rail and the old one in parallel. The operating cost of the tokenized rail may be a fraction of the legacy cost, but the issuer pays for both until the legacy system can actually be switched off. So the near-term economics are not "1% of the old cost." They are "101%," the new system layered on the old. Add unresolved regulatory fragmentation and thin secondary-market liquidity, and the cold-start problem is clear: nobody wants to be the first to bear the double cost.3

The opex of running on tokenized rails is dramatically better, which is exactly why the destination is certain, but you still have to run the whole existing thing in the meantime, which is exactly why the path is slow.
Section 4

The position: build the rails, do not assume the volume

The institutional move is to build the tokenized settlement and collateral infrastructure now, while being honest that the volume arrives only when the transition cost falls. Anchorage's Atlas settlement and collateral network scaled roughly fourfold in a year to nearly 600 participants, and its acquisition of the Securitize for Advisors platform added a tokenization franchise.56 That is the right posture: own the rail, custody the assets, and let the volume migrate as issuers reach the point where they can finally turn the old system off. The mistake is the brochure version that assumes the trillions arrive on their own. They do not. The institution that builds the regulated rail and tells the truth about the transition is the one that is there when the volume finally moves.
