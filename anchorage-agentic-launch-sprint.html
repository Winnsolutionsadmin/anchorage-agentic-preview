Agentic Launch Sprint · v5 · Confidential review build

Who banks
the agents?

A two-week, multi-voice launch that makes Anchorage the name attached to one question: agents are becoming economic actors, and what lets them act is regulated financial rails. The whole sprint argues that banking an agent is a controls problem (segmented accounts, spending limits, Know Your Agent, a human on the hook), not an AI problem.

2 weeks · Mon-Fri x2 Week 1 Jun 22-26 Week 2 Jun 29 - Jul 3 Launch day T-0 = Thu Jun 25 Funnel end → Agentic Banking waitlist
Logistics master (Google Sheet) Action items (7 topics)

Read it top to bottom: a belief-anchored phase map (Hype to Launch to Convert), then the Launch Calendar as a wide grid that scrolls right, the six subsprints (SS-1 to SS-6) that feed the funnel, a content library of the live Google Docs, the partner overview, the conversion rationale, and the action items. Every working doc linked here is a live Google Doc or Sheet, open for comment. Engagement figures across this build are illustrative projections, not performance claims.

Agentic Banking, Nathan McCauley
Hero video cold open · raw selfie to camera · about 60 to 90 seconds
[to camera]

Hey. Nathan here. No script, no slides. I wanted to say this one directly.

Today is the start of a new frontier for Anchorage. We are pivoting hard into an agentic organization.

[beat]

Here is the why. For a while now I have kept saying the same thing. Agents are becoming real economic actors. Right now most of them can advise you. What they still cannot do is act. Hold money. Get paid. Be told no.

And the thing that lets them act is not better AI. It is financial rails.

[beat]

So the question I keep coming back to is simple. Who banks the agents?

When an agent pays the wrong party at three in the morning, who picks up the phone. And what can they actually do about it. If the honest answer is no one, then you do not have a bank for agents. You have an agent with a card and crossed fingers.

[to camera]

Here is what most people get wrong. They think this is an AI problem. It is mostly a controls problem.

Instead of handing an agent your whole bank account, you give it a segmented one. An allowance. A spending limit. A verified merchant on the other side. A human who is still accountable.

We have spent years inside the regulatory perimeter building exactly those boring, hard parts. Custody. Controls. Knowing precisely who is on the other side of an account. That turns out to be the real work of banking the agents.

[beat]

That is what Know Your Agent is about. Banks already do Know Your Customer and Know Your Business. We are taking it one step further. KYA. We do not just underwrite the human who owns the agent. We underwrite the agent itself. Its history. Its identity. A shared understanding of what it is and what it is allowed to do.

This is not meant to be one company holding the whole thing. The industry is better when it is built in the open, by a lot of people.

So we are putting our thinking out this week. And we are opening a waitlist on our agentic page, for the people building where agents meet money. If that is you, come find us. Get on the list.

Partner overview

Why a partner makes this work, the strategic advantage, what we are asking, and the invitation into the paper. Anchored to Ribbit-the-VC and Micky Malka at the thesis level. The whole argument is more credible when it comes from two seats at once: Anchorage builds the rails, Ribbit has spent its career backing new kinds of accounts.

Partner briefing and invitation (Google Doc)
Why this supports the sprint
The whole sprint argues one thing: agents are becoming economic actors, and what lets them act is regulated financial rails, not a smarter model. That argument is far more credible from two seats at once. Anchorage is the chartered bank that builds the rails. Ribbit is the fintech investor that has spent its career backing new kinds of accounts. When both arrive at the same thesis independently, the market reads it as an industry shift, not one bank's marketing.

The strategic advantage

Independent validation
A regulator-fluent, top-tier fintech investor co-signing the thesis turns a brand claim into a category signal.
Distribution Anchorage cannot buy
Ribbit reaches a fintech-native founder and operator audience Anchorage has never had direct access to. The partner's network is what opens the waitlist to that audience.
Lineage and authority
Micky Malka can tell the story Anchorage cannot tell about itself: every wave of finance opens with a new kind of account, and the agent account is the next one. That framing makes the launch feel inevitable.
Genuine, not paid
Because each side speaks from its own book, the alignment reads as real. Anchorage owns the controls and the charter; Ribbit owns the investor lineage and the trust thesis.

What we are asking Ribbit to do

  1. Co-sign the research paper. A supporting quote-tweet the day the paper drops, framing the agent account as the next account in the lineage of finance.
  2. Speak from Malka's seat. One or two original posts plus quote-tweets in Micky's own voice on the thesis. All copy is suggested and is approved or reworded by the partner before posting.
  3. Sit for the live fireside. A roughly 25 to 30 minute live conversation between Nathan and Micky on the agent economy and regulated rails, with a neutral moderator who keeps it substantive.
  4. Route the network to the waitlist. At launch, a note to the founders and companies Ribbit has backed, pointing them to the agentic landing page. Portfolio companies, not any coin community.
Asking Ribbit into the paper
We want Ribbit's fingerprints on the thesis, not just an endorsement of it. The invitation asks Micky and the firm to review the draft of "Who Banks the Agents" and, if they want, contribute a short perspective or a named quote. A co-developed reference text is stronger than a solo paper with a co-sign bolted on, and it gives Ribbit a real stake in the launch. The ask is light and optional: feedback is welcome, participation is welcome, and neither is a condition of the partnership.

06

Conversion

The conversion mechanic for the launch: run a waitlist signup, not a design-partner application.

Anchorage wants to move consumer-facing, and a waitlist is the cleanest instrument for that next chapter: it measures excitement, captures intent at the moment of peak attention, and builds a remarketing list the team owns and can re-engage long after launch week. The whole arc is built to generate action before the audience fully understands the product, and a waitlist is the only ask that converts that early energy instead of gating it behind a sales conversation. Every thread, the paper, the partner posts, the press, and both live interviews route to one landing page and one list.
Waitlist signup · the ask
Asks only that you raise your hand. Lowest bar, widest top of funnel, exactly what you want while hype is doing the work. Converts early energy and builds a list you can remarket to.
Design-partner CTA · not this
Asks for commitment and qualification, which raises the bar and shrinks the top of the funnel. Gates early energy behind a sales conversation. Wrong instrument for a consumer-facing move.
