01

Drip to funnel

The sprint is shaped as a drip that resolves into one on-ramp. It is not three identical stages: a wide tease seeds the question for days, the reveal and proof concentrate the argument, and everything narrows to a single waitlist door.

Stage 01 · the long drip
Seed the question, then keep asking it.
All-caps breadcrumbs (WHO BANKS THE AGENTS, KYA, PROOF OF AGENT) open the field for days with no context. The CEO and the brand handle ask the same four words in one voice, so it reads as a staked position. KOLs react in public, which turns a slogan into a conversation.
runs Jun 22 → ongoing  ·  widest top of funnel
02
Stage 02 · concentrate
Resolve it into a thesis.
The raw selfie reveal, the research paper, and the Ribbit / Malka co-sign turn the tease into a citable argument with named backers. Proof week shows the controls: allowance not wallet, the 3am test, agents run internally.
03
Stage 03 · one door
Route everything to the waitlist.
Press breaks the story, the waitlist opens, and the live fireside airs. Every thread, KOL boost, partner post, and the paper points to one agentic landing page and one signup, framed as a builder signal to lower the bar and raise intent.
End state: every breadcrumb, paper post, partner co-sign, and press hit routes to the agentic landing page and the Agentic Banking waitlist. The waitlist is the single conversion event the whole sprint is built to produce.

Narrative
+ subs
There is a question the entire agent industry has skipped, and a credible institution is the first to ask it out loud.
Sub-1: Agents are about to become economic actors, and the agent stack assumes a money layer that does not exist yet.
Sub-2: When a regulated bank and its CEO ask the same four words in one voice, it reads as a staked position, not a stray musing.
The unasked question has a name and a lineage: Know Your Agent is the obvious next leg after Know Your Customer and Know Your Business.
Sub-1: An agent can think; trusting it with money is a different bar, and that bar is a controls-and-controls problem the regulated world already speaks.
Sub-2: The company that coins the vocabulary (KYA, proof of agent) and repeats it from CEO and brand handle is the company that owns the category.
Anchorage just published the reference text for agentic banking, so the question it asked on Monday now has an authoritative, citable answer.
Sub-1: The constraint on the agent economy was never the model; it is the money layer, and the paper makes that reframe rigorous.
Sub-2: When the regulated player publishes the paper, the paper tends to become the playbook the rest of the field cites.
A top-tier, regulator-fluent fintech investor independently arrives at the same thesis, so this is an industry shift, not one bank's marketing.
Sub-1: Every wave of finance opens with a new kind of account; the agent account is the next one, and the smart money is already naming it.
Sub-2: Anchorage gets distribution into a fintech-native audience it never had on its own, and the alignment reads as genuine because each side speaks from its own seat.
Banking an agent is the boring, hard part (segmented accounts, spending limits, verified merchants, a human on the hook), and boring is exactly the moat.
Sub-1: You give an agent an allowance, not your account; a capped wallet bounds the loss but does not govern the behavior.
Sub-2: When an agent pays the wrong party at 3am, someone has to pick up, and only a regulated, accountable counterparty can credibly be that someone.
The story is now newsworthy enough to brief the press under embargo, and a named regulated bank is the on-record source.
Sub-1: A co-authored thesis plus a chartered counterparty is a real story, and the embargo letter frames it for journalists before it breaks.
Sub-2: A live Nathan-and-Malka fireside is on the calendar, which signals the partnership is substantive and public, not a paid placement.
Independent press confirms Anchorage as the first regulated bank to stand up agentic banking, moving the thesis from Anchorage's own posts to third-party record.
Sub-1: The charter is the part a competitor cannot copy with a funding round, and the coverage leads with it.
Sub-2: Earned coverage plus KOL amplification means the idea has escaped the brand and become a topic the industry is discussing.
The month-long arc resolves into a product on-ramp: the Agentic Banking waitlist is open, and joining is how a builder signals they are building where agents meet money.
Sub-1: This is not a better model; it is a regulated place for an agent to hold and spend under rules, with KYA at the core.
Sub-2: The waitlist is framed as a builder signal rather than a product drop, which lowers the bar to join and raises intent.
Real demand is surfacing in public, and Anchorage is co-designing the category with the builders who are raising their hand.
Sub-1: Direct builder prompts ('if your agent had its own account and limits, what would you build') turn the replies into a live roadmap of demand.
Sub-2: Anchorage even hires agent-first: the agentic interview process is the proof that the company practices the future it is selling.
Anchorage led a sequenced, multi-party movement to a credible launch, and the close credits the partners and reframes the whole sprint as one deliberate plan.
Sub-1: Naming the partners signals a roster of real backers, and named partners are how an emerging category gets legitimized.
Sub-2: Read the month back and the breadcrumb-to-paper-to-partner-to-waitlist sequence was a real campaign, which tells the market Anchorage executes.

Hey. Nathan here. No script, no slides. I wanted to say this one directly.

Today is the start of a new frontier for Anchorage. We are pivoting hard into an agentic organization.

[beat]

Here is the why.

For a while now I have kept saying the same thing. Agents are becoming real economic actors. Right now most of them can advise you. What they still cannot do is act. Hold money. Get paid. Be told no.

And the thing that lets them act is not better AI. It is financial rails.

[beat]

So the question I keep coming back to is simple.

Who banks the agents?

When an agent pays the wrong party at three in the morning, who picks up the phone. And what can they actually do about it. If the honest answer is no one, then you do not have a bank for agents. You have an agent with a card and crossed fingers.

[to camera]

Here is what most people get wrong. They think this is an AI problem. It is mostly a controls problem.

Instead of handing an agent your whole bank account, you give it a segmented one. An allowance. A spending limit. A verified merchant on the other side. A human who is still accountable.

We have spent years inside the regulatory perimeter building exactly those boring, hard parts. Custody. Controls. Knowing precisely who is on the other side of an account. That turns out to be the real work of banking the agents.

[beat]

That is what Know Your Agent is about.

Banks already do Know Your Customer and Know Your Business. We are taking it one step further. KYA. We do not just underwrite the human who owns the agent. We underwrite the agent itself. Its history. Its identity. A shared understanding of what it is and what it is allowed to do.

KYC was for people. KYA is for the things people send to act for them.

[beat]

This is not meant to be one company holding the whole thing. The industry is better when it is built in the open, by a lot of people.

So we are putting our thinking out this week. And we are opening a waitlist on our agentic page, for the people building where agents meet money.

If that is you, come find us. Get on the list.

This is the start of something. And I would rather build it in the open.
