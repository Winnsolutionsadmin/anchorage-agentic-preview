Anchorage Research · Paper 02 · Pillar 2 — Stablecoins

The Issuer War Is the Wrong War

The case for neutral, regulated settlement.

The market is fighting over which stablecoin wins. The institutions that matter are not choosing a brand. They are choosing a settlement layer. Neutrality, not a house token, is the institutional product. Prepared for Nathan McCauley / Anchorage Digital Research.

Executive Summary

The popular framing of the stablecoin market is a contest between issuers: Tether against Circle, the incumbent against the challenger, with market-cap share as the scoreboard. That framing is a distraction. The institutions adopting stablecoins are not picking a favorite token. They are adopting a settlement rail, and what they want from a rail is neutrality, regulatory standing, and the ability to move dollars at any hour. The winner of the issuer war is not the question that decides the market. The question is who provides the regulated, neutral infrastructure underneath all of them.

Key Finding

Stablecoins crossed from speculative instrument to settlement infrastructure in 2025 and 2026. The institutional decision is not which issuer to back; it is which regulated settlement layer to build on.

Section 01

Stablecoins are now settlement infrastructure

The volume tells the story. Chainalysis estimated $28 trillion in real, adjusted stablecoin economic volume in 2025, growing at a 133% annual rate since 2023.[1] Circle reported USDC on-chain transaction volume of $21.5 trillion in the first quarter of 2026 alone, up 263% year over year, with circulation up 28% to $77 billion.[2] a16z described 2025 as "the year the world came onchain," with stablecoins rivaling the major card networks by volume.[3]

The use cases are now concrete and institutional. Western Union launched USDPT, a federally regulated dollar stablecoin issued by Anchorage Digital Bank on Solana, as a 24/7 settlement alternative to SWIFT across its network of more than 550,000 agent locations.[4] State Street and Galaxy launched a tokenized liquidity fund that settles in PayPal's stablecoin.[5] Visa and Mastercard route and settle in stablecoins directly.[6] This is treasury and payments infrastructure, not a trading chip.

Section 02

The market is concentrated, but the institutional question is neutrality

Concentration is real. Tether's USDT held roughly $188 billion and about 59% of stablecoin supply, with USDC the clear second near $76 billion, so the two together account for roughly 82% of the market, not the 93% figure that circulates in secondary summaries.[7] USDC's transaction volume is now gaining on, and by some measures passing, USDT's.[2]

But for an institution, the share contest is the wrong lens. A bank settling client flows, a fund moving subscriptions and redemptions, a payments company bridging currencies, none of these wants to bet its rail on the outcome of a branding war. It wants a settlement layer it can use regardless of which token is on top, backed by reserves it can verify, under a regulator it recognizes. That is the institutional product: neutral, regulated settlement. Nathan McCauley's stated view is that increasing neutrality, rather than pushing any single house stablecoin, is the position that serves an institution best.

Key Finding

Market-cap share answers a question institutions are not asking. They are not buying a token; they are buying settlement. The durable institutional position is neutral and regulated, not brand-committed.

Section 03

Regulation made the rail real

The neutrality thesis only works because the rail is now regulated. The GENIUS Act, the first US federal framework for payment stablecoins, was signed into law in July 2025, requiring full reserve backing in cash and short-term Treasuries, monthly public reserve disclosures, and issuance limited to regulated entities.[8] The OCC issued its implementing proposed rule in February 2026.[9] Reserve custody is itself professionalizing: U.S. Bank, a custodian with more than $11.7 trillion in assets under custody and administration, was selected to hold the reserves backing Anchorage's payment stablecoins.[10] BlackRock's 2026 outlook reframed stablecoins as financial-system infrastructure, "the bridge between traditional finance and digital liquidity," in the words of its global head of market development.[11]

A note on attribution. Anchorage describes itself as the first federally chartered stablecoin issuer under the GENIUS Act and markets USDtb as America's first federally regulated stablecoin.[12] Those are Anchorage's own characterizations, presented as such.

Section 04

The position: be the neutral, regulated layer

The strategic conclusion is to stop fighting the issuer war and provide the ground it is fought on. A federally chartered trust bank can issue, custody reserves for, and settle multiple stablecoins on a neutral, regulated basis. That is more valuable, and more durable, than betting the franchise on one token's market share. As agents begin to pay agents, the demand for a neutral, regulated, always-on dollar will only grow, because an autonomous payment system cannot be tied to the commercial fortunes of a single issuer.

The position

The right product is the settlement layer. The right posture is neutral. The right operator is a bank.
