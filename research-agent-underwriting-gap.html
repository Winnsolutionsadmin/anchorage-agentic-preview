Anchorage Research · Paper 01

The Agent Underwriting Gap

What an agent needs before it touches money.

An Anchorage research paper. Pillar 1 — Financial infrastructure / agentic banking (the AI spine). Prepared for Nathan McCauley, Co-Founder and CEO, Anchorage Digital.

Draft · Not for publication DRAFT for Nathan McCauley / Anchorage Digital Research. Not for publication.
Thesis
The position

The standards for the agent economy establish identity and authorization. They stop, by design, short of underwriting, custody, and accountability. Those are a bank's job.

Executive Summary

The agent economy is being built on a stack of new standards, and they are good. They give an autonomous agent a verifiable identity, a record of what it is permitted to do, and a way to pay. What they do not give it, by the explicit design of their own authors, is the thing regulated finance is built on: an answer to who underwrote the agent, who custodies the assets it acts on, and who is accountable when it fails.

That is the agent underwriting gap. It is not a flaw in the standards. It is the place where the protocol layer ends and the banking layer begins.

Key Finding

Every leading agent-identity standard scopes itself to identity, authorization, and auditability, and explicitly excludes underwriting, credit, and custody. The gap is structural, and it is a banking function.

This paper maps what the standards cover, shows where they stop, and argues that the institutions deploying capital-bearing agents will require a regulated trust layer underneath them, the same way institutional crypto required regulated custody before the capital arrived.

Section 01

The standards are real, and converging fast

Agents are already transacting at scale. Coinbase's x402 payment protocol crossed more than 100 million cumulative agent transactions on the Base network in roughly three quarters, and its transaction mix shifted decisively toward real economic payments, with payments above one dollar rising from 49% to 95% of volume.1 In April 2026, x402 moved to neutral governance under the Linux Foundation, with founding members spanning Circle, Visa, Mastercard, American Express, Google, Microsoft, Amazon Web Services, Stripe, Cloudflare, Shopify, and the Solana and Polygon ecosystems.2 Ethereum's ERC-8004 standard for agent identity went live on mainnet in January 2026, authored by contributors from MetaMask, the Ethereum Foundation, Google, and Coinbase.3 Mastercard completed its first live agentic payment in September 2025 and extended the capability across all US cardholders by November.4

This is not a speculative future. It is an infrastructure build-out with the largest names in payments and technology behind it.

Section 02

Where the standards stop

Read the standards on their own terms and a consistent boundary appears.

Ethereum's ERC-8004 defines three registries, for identity, reputation, and validation, and its authors state plainly that the protocol "does not cover payments," deliberately leaving application logic off-chain.5 The identity-verification incumbents Sumsub and Trulioo both define Know Your Agent as a framework for identity, authorization, and auditability, and both explicitly exclude underwriting, credit decisions, and asset custody from its scope.6 Visa's Trusted Agent Protocol confirms that an agent is authorized to act for a consumer; it is authentication, not custody.7 The foundational MIT Media Lab work on authenticated delegation, from January 2025, proposes extending existing identity protocols to agents and does not even use the phrase "Know Your Agent."8

Two facts follow. First, Know Your Agent is a contested category, used independently by academic, vendor, payments-network, and protocol claimants. No single firm coined it, and Anchorage does not claim to.6 Second, and more important, every one of these standards answers the same two questions, who is this agent and what is it allowed to do, and none answers the three a regulated institution must answer before it lets an agent touch capital: who underwrote it, who custodies what it touches, and who is accountable when it fails. As of June 2026, no financial regulator has issued a Know Your Agent standard.6

Key Finding

The protocols establish identity and authorization. Underwriting, custody, and accountability are out of their scope on purpose. That is the gap a chartered bank fills.

Section 03

Why the gap matters now

The economic pull is real and it is being quantified by the most conservative institutions in finance. McKinsey estimates agentic AI could reduce banking operating costs by up to 20% and open a four-percentage-point return-on-tangible-equity gap between leaders and laggards.9 Citi argues agentic AI could affect finance more than the internet did.10 Gartner projects 40% of enterprise applications will embed task-specific AI agents by the end of 2026, up from under 5% a year earlier.11

The same sources flag the discipline problem. Gartner predicts more than 40% of agentic AI projects will be canceled by the end of 2027, citing cost, unclear value, and weak risk controls.12 The agent economy is arriving, and it is arriving unevenly. The agents that get near real money will be the ones a regulated institution can stand behind.

Section 04

The position: underwrite the agent

The regulated-bank reading of Know Your Agent is the layer the standards leave open. It is not the identity credential, which the protocols already issue. It is underwriting the agent before it transacts, custodying the assets it acts on under segmented, governed, recoverable controls (an allowance, not the keys), and carrying the accountability a charter requires.

Anchorage's position is that these controls are not friction on the agent economy. They are its on-ramp. An agent that a regulated bank can underwrite and stand behind is an agent an institution can actually deploy against real capital. The identity standards are necessary. They are not sufficient. Sufficiency is a bank.
