Sprint 1Agentic Banking Launch
Jun 23 to Jul 4

The agentic banking launch. Agents are becoming economic actors; what lets them act is regulated rails. The one agentic-led sprint, compressing phases 1 to 6 into the launch with the waitlist and the agentic recruiting sub-campaign (the hiring and triage bot) as a parallel hype lane that drives builders to the waitlist.

Why it aligns with Nathan (verbatim)

“Agentic Banking is the bridge between those two worlds: a system that brings trust, governance and real financial rails to autonomous systems.”

Nathan McCauley, Consensus 2026 (S24, audio-verify before use)

Aligns to S24 (agentic banking), his on-record founding definition. It is also his newest and thinnest belief by the data, which is exactly why this is the one sprint that leads agentic: the durable, on-voice substance underneath it is KYA and the allowance model, not capability claims.

The research paper
"Who Banks the Agents?" research paper

A macro gap doc: every competitor is building a piece of the agent's wallet; none is building the agent's bank. The bottleneck is not smarter models, it is a funded, regulated, identified, accountable place for an agent's money.

Why this paper: It is principle-grade and costs zero product substance, it gives press a citeable artifact, and it reframes the whole category around a question Anchorage gets to answer. Highest leverage, lowest risk in the sprint.

The interview is with Ribbit Capital (Micky Malka, @mickymalka / @RibbitCapital), a long-time Anchorage backer, talking founder-to-investor about why the agent economy needs a chartered trust anchor. Friendly-only, as an investor; keep it category-level, no capability claims.

Sprint 3A Thousand Things to Get Right
Jul 21 to Aug 1

A thousand things to get right. Security is a design property, not a patch backlog; transparency plus federal oversight is best-in-breed trust. The accountability wedge, and the empirically winning angle.

Why it aligns with Nathan (verbatim)

“There's a thousand things to get right. There's not one. It only takes one thing to get wrong.”

Nathan McCauley, on security as discipline (S4)

Aligns to S4 (security-first, conviction 90), S34 (secure-by-default, CLEAN) and S11 (transparency plus oversight). This is his deepest engineering conviction and, by the data, the highest-performing angle in the whole field: the single best AI post across competitors was a security and accountability story.

The research paper
"A Thousand Things to Get Right" research paper

Security as a design property: you win by obliterating bug classes and removing single points of failure, not by patching faster. Transparency plus federal oversight is the best-in-breed level of trust, and that is what an autonomous-money system needs.

Why this paper: It is the empirically winning angle, it is Anchorage's home turf, and it is uncontested: rivals brag about agent capability, no one owns who is accountable when an agent gets it wrong.

The live interview
Concept + candidates

A security-and-trust conversation: who is accountable when an autonomous system fails, and what a chartered bank does differently.

Candidates: Google Cloud (@GoogleCloud, existing partner, friendly-only, the secure-infra beat), a16z (@a16z, partner, friendly-only), plus a named security researcher to source (the relationship index is exec-heavy, so we add a credible independent security voice). Keep all partners friendly-only.

Two sides of the same token. Regulation is the chosen path, not the burden; fair, open access to banking is a right. Nathan's single strongest CLEAN belief. Agentic flare: the rulebook for autonomous money.

Why it aligns with Nathan (verbatim)

“Crypto and regulation are two sides of the same token.”

Nathan McCauley, 2026 (S2)

Aligns to S2 (regulation-as-chosen, frequency 92, conviction 96, CLEAN), his strongest belief and the safest to say, plus S6 (debanking) and S17 (fair access). He has on-record advocacy here (the FIRM Act, Senate testimony), and the framing is bipartisan.

The research paper
"Two Sides of the Same Token" research paper

Regulation and innovation are not opposed; the right move is to volunteer for the highest scrutiny. Fair, open access to banking is a right, and the fix for debanking is structural: due process, written explanations, appeals.

Why this paper: It is his strongest CLEAN belief (no forward-looking claim needed), it is bipartisan and durable across administrations, and it earns media because a chartered-bank CEO arguing for scrutiny is rare and credible.

The Position

Anchorage is the platform for agentic accountability.

The one line the whole campaign drives. Every competitor is building a piece of the agent's wallet; Anchorage is the only one that can be the agent's bank. The contest is not capability, it is accountability, and that is a chartered-bank advantage.

As agents become economic actors, what lets them act is regulated financial rails: a place to hold value, a way to move it, an identity for the agent, and controls that make all of it auditable. That is the trust layer for autonomous finance, a bank problem before it is an AI problem, and the load-bearing qualifier is regulated. Anchorage is the only fully operational federally chartered bank making the claim, which is why the campaign can plant one flag and defend it: the platform for agentic accountability, the place a builder, a bank, or a regulator trusts an autonomous agent to touch money.
